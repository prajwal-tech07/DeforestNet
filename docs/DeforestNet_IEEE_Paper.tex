\begin{abstract}
Tropical deforestation continues at an unprecedented rate, with over 6.7 million
hectares lost in 2024 alone. Existing monitoring systems predominantly perform
binary forest/non-forest classification, failing to distinguish \emph{why} forest
cover is being lost---an essential input for targeted enforcement and conservation
policy. This paper presents \textbf{DeforestNet}, an end-to-end satellite
intelligence platform that fuses Sentinel-1 Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR) and
Sentinel-2 multi-spectral imagery into a single 11-band input stack and performs
pixel-wise semantic segmentation across \textbf{six deforestation cause classes}:
intact Forest, Logging, Mining, Agriculture encroachment, Fire damage, and
Infrastructure expansion. A modified U-Net with a ResNet-34 encoder (24.4M
parameters) is trained using a combined Cross-Entropy + Dice + Focal loss to
handle severe class imbalance. On a synthetic 2,800-sample benchmark the model
achieves \textbf{99.58\% overall accuracy}, \textbf{97.08\% mean IoU}, and
\textbf{98.49\% mean Dice coefficient}. Beyond inference, DeforestNet integrates
a real-time alert pipeline, a three-tier notification system (Firebase FCM,
Telegram, Gmail SMTP), and a Flask-based interactive web dashboard for operational
deployment. Grad-CAM explainability overlays provide per-band attribution
heatmaps. The entire system is open-source, cloud-deployable, and operates at
zero data-acquisition cost using the ESA Copernicus free-tier API.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
% ============================================================

Tropical forests cover approximately 12\% of the Earth land surface yet harbour
more than 50\% of all terrestrial species and store the equivalent of 25 years of
global fossil-fuel CO$_{2}$ emissions~\cite{pan2011large}. The Food and Agriculture
Organisation (FAO) estimates that \textbf{10 million hectares} of forest are lost
annually, with the pace accelerating in Brazil, the Democratic Republic of Congo,
and Southeast Asia~\cite{fao2020global}. The EU Deforestation Regulation (EUDR,
Regulation (EU) 2023/1115), mandating supply-chain traceability to the parcel
level for seven high-risk commodities, comes into full effect in December 2026,
creating urgent industrial demand for accurate, cause-specific deforestation
intelligence~\cite{eudr2023}.

Current operational systems such as PRODES, Global Forest Watch (GFW), and
JAXA PALSAR classify every pixel as one of two classes: forested or
deforested~\cite{shimada2014newglobal}. This binary view is inadequate for
enforcement agencies that must distinguish legal agricultural expansion from
illegal mining or uncontrolled wildfire. Furthermore, pure optical systems are
blind to 60--80\% of tropical forest pixels during peak deforestation seasons
due to persistent cloud cover~\cite{asner2009highresolution}, necessitating
SAR integration.

We address these gaps with \textbf{DeforestNet}, making three principal
contributions:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{11-Band SAR-Optical Fusion.} We combine Sentinel-1 VV/VH SAR
        backscatter with Sentinel-2 B2, B3, B4, B8 optical bands and five
        derived spectral indices (NDVI, EVI, SAVI, VV/VH Ratio, RVI) into a
        unified 11-channel input stack, enabling all-weather monitoring.
  \item \textbf{Six-Class Cause Identification.} We define a deforestation
        taxonomy---Forest, Logging, Mining, Agriculture, Fire,
        Infrastructure---and train a U-Net + ResNet-34 encoder to perform
        pixel-wise multi-class segmentation.
  \item \textbf{Operational Platform.} DeforestNet wraps inference inside a
        production REST API with automated monitoring, three-tier notifications,
        interactive map dashboard, and Grad-CAM explainability.
\end{enumerate}

The paper is structured as follows: Section~\ref{sec:related} reviews related
work; Section~\ref{sec:data} describes the 11-band input; Section~\ref{sec:model}
details the U-Net model; Section~\ref{sec:training} presents training methodology;
Section~\ref{sec:results} reports results; Section~\ref{sec:system} covers the
operational system; and Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Binary Forest Mapping}
Early deep learning approaches applied fully convolutional networks
(FCN)~\cite{long2015fully} and SegNet~\cite{badrinarayanan2017segnet} to
single-date Landsat composites. While achieving high binary accuracy ($>$95\%),
they fail to localise individual disturbance agents~\cite{torres2021deforestation}.

\subsection{SAR-Optical Fusion}
Several works have fused Sentinel-1 and Sentinel-2 for land-cover mapping.
Kussul et al.~\cite{kussul2017deep} demonstrated that multi-temporal SAR fusion
improves crop-type F1 by 7--12\%. Rosen et al.~\cite{rosen2021measuring} showed
that C-band SAR is sensitive to surface structural changes caused by logging
and fire.

\subsection{U-Net Architectures}
The U-Net~\cite{ronneberger2015unet} introduced skip connections that preserve
spatial detail during upsampling. Subsequent variants replaced the encoder
with ResNet-34~\cite{he2016deep} or EfficientNet. Chen et
al.~\cite{chen2018encoderdecoder} extended this to DeepLabv3+ with atrous
convolutions. We adopt ResNet-34 for its optimal accuracy--parameter trade-off
on moderate-resolution satellite imagery.

\subsection{Multi-Class Deforestation}
Silva et al.~\cite{silva2021mapping} used a random forest classifier on 12
Landsat bands across 4 classes, achieving 88\% accuracy without SAR bands. Our
six-class approach with an 11-channel deep encoder substantially extends this.

\section{11-Band Satellite Input Architecture}

\subsection{Sentinel-1 SAR Bands (Channels 0--1)}

Sentinel-1 C-band SAR operates at 5.405~GHz ($\lambda{=}5.6$\,cm) in
Interferometric Wide Swath mode at 10\,m ground resolution. Two co-polarisation
channels are used:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{VV}: sensitive to surface roughness. Logging causes a sharp
        backscatter decrease of 3--8\,dB.
  \item \textbf{VH}: cross-polarisation sensitive to canopy volume scattering.
        Intact forest shows high VH; cleared land very low VH.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Sentinel-2 Optical Bands (Channels 2--5)}

We use four 10\,m MSI bands (Table~\ref{tab:s2bands}). Bands B4 and B8 are
the primary discriminators: intact canopy shows low Red and high NIR; cleared
land inverts this. Band B2 enables EVI atmospheric correction.

\section{Model Architecture}
\label{sec:model}
% ============================================================

\subsection{U-Net with ResNet-34 Encoder}

DeforestNet adopts an encoder-decoder architecture based on
U-Net~\cite{ronneberger2015unet}, replacing the vanilla encoder with a
ResNet-34 backbone~\cite{he2016deep} modified to accept 11-channel input.
Skip connections bridge encoder and decoder feature maps at four spatial
scales, preserving fine-grained boundary detail. The network has
\textbf{24,454,534 parameters} (93.3\,MB).

\subsection{Encoder: Modified ResNet-34}

The ResNet-34 first convolution is remapped from
$7{\times}7{\times}3{\to}64$ to $7{\times}7{\times}11{\to}64$.
Table~\ref{tab:encoder} lists the five encoder stages.

\begin{table}[!t]
  \caption{ResNet-34 Encoder Feature Map Dimensions}
  \label{tab:encoder}
  \centering
  \small
  \begin{tabular}{l c c c}
    \toprule
    \textbf{Stage} & \textbf{Blocks} & \textbf{Channels} & \textbf{Spatial} \\
    \midrule
    Stem + MaxPool & 1 & 64  & $128{\times}128$ \\
    Layer 1        & 3 & 64  & $64{\times}64$   \\
    Layer 2        & 4 & 128 & $32{\times}32$   \\
    Layer 3        & 6 & 256 & $16{\times}16$   \\
    Bottleneck     & 3 & 512 & $8{\times}8$     \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

\subsection{Decoder: Skip-Connection Upsampling}

Each decoder block bilinearly upsamples by $2{\times}$, concatenates the
skip connection, and applies two $3{\times}3$ conv layers with batch
normalisation and ReLU. A $1{\times}1$ head with dropout ($p{=}0.2$)
produces the final six-class logit map at $256{\times}256$.

    Decoder 3 & 128 & $384\to128$ & $32{\times}32$ \\
    Decoder 2 & 64  & $192\to64$  & $64{\times}64$ \\
    Decoder 1 & 64  & $128\to32$  & $128{\times}128$ \\
    Head      & --  & $32\to6$    & $256{\times}256$ \\

\section{Training Methodology}
\label{sec:training}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Dataset}

We generated a synthetic 2,800-sample dataset of spatially realistic 11-band
$256{\times}256$ tiles using procedurally generated spectral signatures per class.
The train/val/test split is 2,000 / 400 / 400. Synthetic generation enables
controlled study of class distribution and band contribution without dependence
on licensed satellite archives during development.

\subsection{Class Imbalance and Weighting}

Forest typically covers 60--75\% of pixels while Infrastructure and Mining are
rare ($<$3\%). Inverse-frequency class weights are computed as:

\begin{equation}
  w_c = \frac{N_{\text{total}}}{C \cdot N_c}
  \label{eq:classweights}
\end{equation}

\noindent yielding $\mathbf{w}{=}[0.04,\,0.65,\,0.99,\,1.56,\,0.74,\,2.02]$
for [Forest, Logging, Mining, Agriculture, Fire, Infrastructure].

\subsection{Combined Loss Function}

\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L} = 0.5\,\mathcal{L}_{\text{CE}} + 0.3\,\mathcal{L}_{\text{Dice}}
                + 0.2\,\mathcal{L}_{\text{Focal}}
  \label{eq:loss}
\end{equation}

\begin{align}
  \mathcal{L}_{\text{CE}}    &= -\sum_c w_c\,y_c\log\hat{y}_c
  \label{eq:ce}\\[3pt]
  \mathcal{L}_{\text{Dice}}  &= 1 - \frac{2\sum_c |\hat{y}_c \cap y_c|}
                                         {\sum_c (|\hat{y}_c|+|y_c|)}
  \label{eq:dice}\\[3pt]
  \mathcal{L}_{\text{Focal}} &= -\sum_c w_c\,(1-\hat{y}_c)^{\gamma}\,y_c\log\hat{y}_c
  \label{eq:focal}
\end{align}

with $\gamma{=}2.0$. The Dice term directly optimises the IoU metric; the Focal
term emphasises rare hard-to-classify regions.

\subsection{Data Augmentation}

On-the-fly augmentation includes: random horizontal/vertical flips, $90^\circ$
rotation, brightness jitter ($\pm$0.1), contrast scaling ($\times$0.9--1.1),
additive Gaussian noise ($\sigma{=}0.02$), and random band dropout ($p{=}0.05$).
All transforms are applied consistently to image and mask tensors.

\subsection{Three-Tier Notification System}

Upon generating a \emph{High} or \emph{Critical} alert, notifications are
dispatched concurrently through three tiers:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Firebase FCM} (Tier~1): Sub-second mobile push notification to
        field officers. Free tier: 500k messages/month.
  \item \textbf{Telegram Bot} (Tier~2): HTML-formatted message with coordinates,
        severity, class thumbnail, and map link. Unlimited and free.
  \item \textbf{Gmail SMTP} (Tier~3): Full HTML report with Grad-CAM overlays
        and GPS coordinates. 500 emails/day free; automatic failover.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Grad-CAM Explainability}

Grad-CAM~\cite{selvaraju2017gradcam} attribution maps are computed at the final
decoder convolutional layer. For each class $c$:

\begin{equation}
  \alpha_k^c = \frac{1}{Z}\sum_{i,j} \frac{\partial y^c}{\partial A^k_{ij}}
  \label{eq:gradcam_weights}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
  L_{\text{Grad-CAM}}^c = \mathrm{ReLU}\!\left(\sum_k \alpha_k^c A^k\right)
  \label{eq:gradcam_map}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $A^k$ denotes the $k$-th feature map activation. Heatmaps are
overlaid on the NIR false-colour composite and included in email alerts.

\subsection{Automated Monitoring Scheduler}

The \texttt{AutoMonitor} module runs a background thread triggering inference
on new tile acquisitions at a configurable interval (default: 120\,s in demo
mode). Alerts are persisted to SQLite and notifications dispatched without
manual intervention.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Strengths}

The 11-band fusion architecture enables all-weather monitoring through SAR bands
unaffected by cloud cover. The combined CE+Dice+Focal loss effectively handles
severe class imbalance, evidenced by the strong Infrastructure IoU (96.4\%) despite
low natural prevalence. Operating entirely on free ESA Copernicus data keeps
data-acquisition cost at zero.

\subsection{Limitations and Future Work}

\textbf{Synthetic data:} The training corpus is entirely synthetic.
Domain-adaptive transfer learning~\cite{ganin2016domain} to real Sentinel tiles
is the top engineering priority.

\textbf{Temporal stacking:} Single-date inference cannot distinguish active
burning from burn scars or active from recent logging. Multi-temporal stacking
is the highest-priority research extension.

\textbf{Agriculture ambiguity:} Agriculture (IoU 89.36\%) confuses with secondary
regrowth forest (both at NDVI 0.3--0.5). SWIR bands (B11, B12) and multi-date
NDVI trajectories will improve discrimination.

\textbf{Tile scale:} The $256{\times}256$ tile covers $2.56{\times}2.56$\,km at
10\,m resolution. Sliding-window inference is required for landscape-scale
monitoring.

% ============================================================
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
% ============================================================

We presented \textbf{DeforestNet}, a complete end-to-end satellite intelligence
platform for cause-specific deforestation monitoring. By fusing 11 spectral and
radar bands from the free Sentinel-1 and Sentinel-2 missions and training a
24.4M-parameter U-Net + ResNet-34 on a balanced synthetic dataset, we achieve
\textbf{99.58\% overall accuracy} and \textbf{97.08\% mean IoU} across six
deforestation cause classes. The operational system---Flask REST API, automated
monitoring scheduler, three-tier notification pipeline, interactive web dashboard,
and Grad-CAM explainability---demonstrates that production-grade deforestation
intelligence can be built entirely from open-source, zero-cost components.
DeforestNet directly addresses the binary classification gap in current systems
and is positioned to support EU Deforestation Regulation compliance obligations.

Future work: (1) domain-adaptive fine-tuning on real Sentinel imagery;
(2) multi-temporal stacking; (3) SWIR band integration; (4) containerised
deployment on Google Earth Engine.
